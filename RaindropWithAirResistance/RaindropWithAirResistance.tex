\section{The Problem}

We want to spend some time thinking about raindrops and air resistance.
In particular, we want to find a function $v_y(t)$ which maps time $t$ to the velocity 
of the raindrop. \\

We assume that our mathematical raindrop has both a constant mass and a fixed shape.
The constant mass is important because a change in mass -- a real raindrop might lose some of its water molecules while 
falling through the air -- would lead to a change in terminal velocity
\footnote{ https://journals.ametsoc.org/doi/pdf/10.1175/1520-0450\%281969\%29008\%3C0249\%3ATVORA\%3E2.0.CO\%3B2 }.
\\

Furthermore, we assume that there is no wind and the only forces governing the raindrops movement
are $F_g$ (gravitational pull) and $F_r$ (air resistance)
\\

The air resistance in our model is proportional to $v_y(t)^2$. We introduce constant $k$ to ...

\begin{align} \label{eq:6}
F_r &= k \cdot v_y(t)^2
\end{align}

We expect the velocity
to converge to some terminal value $V_t$.
Thus, we expect the unknown function $v_y(t)$ to rise steep (start of the fall) and then flatten out (converging to terminal velocity).
